Bastion

No, not the one from that online FPS team arena

Fantastic world-building, beautiful art, and a deep-voiced narrator. Bastion's not the most ambitious isometric role-playing action game, but it's so well-executed. Plus I'm a sucker for games with time loops and broken time loops. Supergiant being Supergiant, it has an awesome soundtrack too.

Cook, Serve, Delicious!

THE FOOD'S ON FIRE AND THE TOILET NEEDS FLUSHING

A restaurant sim that's really a cross between Typing of the Dead and Cake Mania. It's way more fun than it has any right to be.

Dishonored

Revenge solved everything, until the second game

Top-notch atmosphere and world building. The powers — ranging from teleportation to possession to stopping time — are a blast to play around with, whether you're playing stealth or going on a murderous rampage. The story explores class struggle and themes of honour in the dreary city of Dunwall, a steampunk reimagining of Victorian-era London. What makes it immersive is that levels and characters respond to your actions. It's a lot more subtle and thoughful than the forced good/evil alignment in other role-playing games.

Empty

A puzzle game made in 72 hours

This was an entry for Ludum Dare 37 with the theme of "One Room". By rotating the room, furniture disappears against a backdrop of a similar colour. It ended up being one of my favourite games in 2016 just for the really clever concept and well-executed controls. If this were a paid game, I'd gladly empty my wallet for it.

Life Is Strange

The game that made me cry at 3am

LIVED UP TO THE HYPE. It was a wild ride from start to finish. This game is a shining example of how the interactive nature of games enhances storytelling and narrative choice.

Mirror's Edge

First person parkour simulator

Years on, Mirror's Edge has become somewhat of a cult classic. People acknowledge the potential lurking beneath the flaws. I first fell in love with the game for its beautiful environments and near-future dystopian aesthetic. And after completing it, I couldn't take movement in first-person games seriously. Because traversal in Mirror's Edge feels fantastic. The camera bobs with every step, every breath, and you feel the weight of your movement. Sadly, not many games have achieved that physicality.

Mirror's Edge Catalyst

Two steps forward, two steps back

The flawed but enjoyable tribute to the game that first captured my heart with its striking near-future aesthetic. What I loved: combat is vastly improved with focus on momentum; Faith's moveset has been expanded for even more possibilities. What didn't work: the free-roam world only sounded nice on paper; the city, despite the stunning visuals, is bland and lifeless. What I tolerated: the story.

You know, I'm just glad we even got a sequel.

Monument Valley

A game of impossible geometries

One of the most unforgettable experiences in mobile gaming. Since its release, many have tried to emulate the aesthetic but failed to capture the wonder of the game.

Portal 1/2

No wonder they're video game classics

Liked the portal puzzles, loved the dark, irreverent humour. Home to the unfortunate "the cake is a lie" meme and also the best ending credits of all time.

Also has the dubious honour of making my friend suffer from motion sickness while playing it.

Refunct

More like refund amirite

If I wanted first-person parkour, I'd play Mirror's Edge... I just don't like this game. What most reviews call "peaceful" and "relaxing", I call unengaging. It didn't add anything to my gaming experience. I did enjoy this AGDQ speedrun though!

The Sims 2

We don't need your weirder stories

It's strange to still be playing The Sims 2 in 2016. But The Sims 4 is fundamentally broken. For all its emphasis on weirder stories, The Sims 4 falls flat in delivering gameplay with depth and sims with diverse personalities. And so, I keep going back to this childhood comfort. The modding community continues to put out amazing content, which means the game has aged gracefully.
